Conformal Coating for PCB Assembly: Material Selection, Application Methods, and When Your Product Needs It

July 17, 2026 · 11 min read

Conformal coating is the PCB assembly step that most products skip — until they can't. A thin polymer film applied to the surface of an assembled circuit board, conformal coating protects against moisture, dust, chemicals, and temperature extremes. For consumer electronics that live indoors, it's optional. For automotive under-hood controllers, marine navigation equipment, outdoor industrial sensors, and medical devices that face sterilization cycles, it's the difference between a product that works for years and one that fails within months of deployment.

This guide covers the five decisions every engineering team faces when specifying conformal coating: whether your product needs it, which material chemistry to choose, which application method fits your production volume, which IPC standards to reference, and how to communicate coating requirements to your PCB assembly partner. Each section draws on real production data from our conformal coating line at Uppcba's Shenzhen facility, where we run both selective automated coating and manual spray/brush applications for prototype through mid-volume programs.

Do You Need Conformal Coating? The Environmental Decision Matrix

Conformal coating adds $0.30–$1.80 per board in material and processing cost. That number is small in absolute terms but represents 2–8% of the assembly cost for a typical mid-complexity board — enough to matter when you're building 5,000 units. The decision comes down to your product's operating environment and the cost of field failure.

The Economics of Coating: Prevention vs. Field Failure

Our production data shows conformal coating adds $0.30–$1.80 per board for a typical 100×80mm PCB. Field failure of an uncoated board in a high-humidity environment carries a cost that is 50–500× higher when you factor in warranty replacement, shipping, labor, and brand damage. For a product shipping 5,000 units with a 2% field failure rate, the math is unambiguous: ~$7,500 in coating cost vs. $25,000–$125,000 in warranty claims at a conservative $250–$1,250 per field return.

Conformal Coating Materials: Chemistry, Strengths, and Trade-offs

Five base chemistries dominate production usage. Each has a distinct profile of chemical resistance, dielectric strength, application viscosity, reworkability, and cost. The choice is never purely technical — it's a triangle of protection level, production throughput, and rework access.

MaterialDielectric Strength (kV/mm)Temp RangeReworkable?Cost ($/L)Best For
Acrylic (AR)18–22−65°C to +125°CYes — dissolves easily in solvent$40–80General-purpose protection; consumer and light industrial electronics where rework access matters
Silicone (SR)15–20−65°C to +200°CDifficult — requires mechanical removal or aggressive solvent soak$90–180High-temperature environments; automotive under-hood, LED lighting, power supplies with hot components
Urethane (UR)20–25−65°C to +125°CVery difficult — chemically resistant by design; usually requires burn-through with soldering iron$60–120Chemical and abrasion resistance; aerospace fuel system controllers, industrial solvent-exposed equipment
Epoxy (ER)20–25−65°C to +150°CNo — effectively permanent; removal damages board$55–100Maximum mechanical protection; military/aerospace where rework is not expected
Parylene (XY)220–280−200°C to +150°CNo — deposited as vapor, requires micro-abrasion to remove$500–1,500Medical implants, MEMS devices, ultra-high-reliability where nanometer-thickness pinhole-free coverage is required

In practice, 70–80% of commercial and industrial PCB assembly programs use acrylic conformal coating. It provides adequate protection for most non-extreme environments at the lowest material cost, and its easy reworkability means boards can be repaired during production without scrapping the assembly. Silicone is the second most common, driven by automotive electronics where component operating temperatures routinely exceed 100°C. Urethane and epoxy are niche choices for specific chemical or mechanical requirements, and parylene is reserved for applications where failure is not an option — pacemakers, satellite subsystems, and deep-sea sensor packages.

What About UV-Curable Coatings?

UV-curable acrylics and urethanes have gained significant production share in the last five years. They cure in seconds under UV lamp exposure rather than the 10–30 minutes required for thermal or moisture-cure chemistries, which directly increases throughput on high-volume SMT lines. The trade-off is shadow curing: areas under components, inside connectors, or in BGA underfill regions that are not exposed to direct UV light may remain uncured. IPC Class 3 requirements often mandate dual-cure formulations (UV + secondary moisture cure) to address this, adding $15–25 per liter to material cost but guaranteeing full cure across the entire board surface.

Application Methods: Throughput vs. Precision

The coating material you choose constrains the application methods available to you — but so do your board geometry, component clearances, and production volume. Four methods dominate production, and the wrong choice can turn a $0.50/board coating process into a $3.00/board one through masking labor alone.

MethodThroughput (boards/hr)Masking Required?Edge DefinitionMaterial WasteBest Volume
Manual Brush5–15Yes — manual tape/peelable mask on connectors, test points, ground padsPoor — ±3mm, operator-dependentLow — 5–10%Prototype, <100 units
Manual Spray (aerosol)10–25Yes — same as brush, tape mask on keep-out zonesFair — ±2mm, overspray unavoidableHigh — 30–50% oversprayPrototype to low-volume, <500 units
Automated Dip60–120Yes — complex masking boots/plugs on connectors, potentiometers, unsealed relaysGood — ±1mm with controlled withdrawal speedMedium — 15–25% (bath contamination over time)Mid-volume, 500–5,000 units
Automated Selective Coating40–80Minimal — program keep-out zones in software; only non-sealable connectors need physical masksExcellent — ±0.5mm with precision needle/dispense valveLow — 5–10%Mid to high volume, 1,000–50,000+ units

Selective coating is the dominant method for production volumes above 1,000 units. A programmed XYZ gantry positions a dispensing needle over the board, applies coating only to specified areas, and skips connectors, test points, and unsealed components automatically. The upfront programming cost (typically 2–4 hours of engineering time to create the coating program from your Gerber data) is amortized across the production run — at 5,000 units, that's roughly $0.05 per board. The real savings come from eliminating manual masking labor, which can cost $0.80–$2.00 per board on a complex assembly with 8–12 connectors and test point arrays.

Masking: The Hidden Cost Driver

On a recent 3,000-unit industrial controller program at our facility, switching from manual spray with tape masking to automated selective coating reduced per-board processing cost from $1.85 to $0.72 — a 61% reduction. The difference was entirely in labor: manual masking took an operator 3.5 minutes per board; the selective coating program eliminated 90% of that time. Material cost actually increased slightly ($0.28 vs. $0.22 per board due to precision dispensing equipment amortization), but the labor savings dominated.

IPC Standards for Conformal Coating: What to Reference in Your Specification

Three IPC standards govern conformal coating in production. Referencing the wrong one — or referencing none — is the most common cause of coating-related quality disputes between OEMs and assembly partners.

StandardWhat It CoversWhen to Reference It
IPC-CC-830Qualification and performance of conformal coating materials — dielectric strength, moisture/insulation resistance (MIR per IPC-TM-650 2.6.3.1), thermal shock endurance, flammability rating (UL 94 V-0 or V-1), fungus resistanceWhen specifying which coating material to use. This is the material qualification standard — it tells your manufacturer "use a coating that meets these electrical, thermal, and chemical performance thresholds," not "use product XYZ from supplier ABC."
IPC-A-610 (Class 2/3)Acceptance criteria for applied coating — coverage completeness, thickness (typically 30–130 μm for acrylic/silicone/urethane, 12–25 μm for parylene), bubble/void limits, delamination, discoloration, fluorescence inspection under UV light, keep-out accuracy on masked areasWhen defining pass/fail criteria for coated assemblies. Specify which IPC class applies: Class 2 for commercial/industrial electronics, Class 3 for high-reliability — the difference matters for coating thickness tolerances and allowable void density.
IPC-HDBK-830Design guide for conformal coating — component standoff height requirements (≥0.25mm for coating to flow under components), connector and test point keep-out zone dimensions, coating-compatible materials selection (some solder mask chemistries inhibit coating adhesion)During DFM review, before layout is finalized. This handbook prevents the most common coating design error: placing keep-out zones too small around connectors, forcing the coating operator to mask impossibly tight tolerances.

The most common specification we see from new clients is: "Apply conformal coating to IPC Class 2." This is incomplete — it specifies the inspection standard but not the material. A proper specification reads: "Apply acrylic conformal coating meeting IPC-CC-830 qualification requirements. Coverage per IPC-A-610 Class 2 acceptance criteria. Keep-out zones per supplied coating drawing (or per IPC-HDBK-830 default clearances if no drawing provided)." This eliminates ambiguity about which material, which application standard, and which inspection criteria apply.

How to Specify Conformal Coating in Your Assembly RFQ

When you send an RFQ for PCB assembly that includes conformal coating, include these five items. Omitting any one of them will generate a clarifying question from your manufacturer — or worse, an assumption that may not match your requirements.

#Specification ItemExample
1Material type"Acrylic conformal coating per IPC-CC-830" (alternatively: "Silicone, high-temperature grade, −65°C to +200°C operating range")
2Coverage area"Both sides, full coverage except keep-out zones marked on Gerber mechanical layer" (or: "Top side only, selective coating per provided coating drawing")
3Thickness"30–80 μm dry film thickness, measured per IPC-A-610 Class 2" (IPC Class 3 tightens this to 30–60 μm typically)
4Keep-out zones"Connectors J1–J4, test point array TP1–TP24, unsealed potentiometer R12, edge connector gold fingers — no coating"
5Inspection method"100% visual inspection under UV light (365nm) for coverage verification on first article; statistical sampling per AQL 1.0 on production units"

If your product is undergoing a design for manufacturability review, this is the right moment to flag conformal coating requirements. A DFM review catches issues like connectors placed too close together for effective masking, tall components that shadow adjacent areas from spray/dip coating, and keep-out zones that are too small for the coating method your production volume will use — fixing these in layout is free; fixing them after fabrication means manual masking labor or board redesign.

Conformal Coating as a Production Decision

Conformal coating is rarely a technical question — the engineering case for protecting electronics in harsh environments is settled. It's a production economics question: does the per-board cost of coating, masking, inspection, and rework access justify itself against the expected cost of field failures in your product's target environment?

For a consumer product that lives on a desk in a climate-controlled office, the answer is usually no. For an automotive sensor mounted behind a wheel arch, exposed to road salt and pressure-washer spray, the answer is unequivocally yes — and the cost of rework for a coated board that needs repair is still cheaper than the warranty cost of a field-failed unit.

The decision becomes more nuanced in the middle ground: industrial equipment in a factory with moderate humidity and occasional washdown, outdoor telecom equipment in a temperate climate, medical devices that see occasional surface disinfection but not full sterilization. In these cases, start with the environmental matrix at the top of this article, identify which failure mechanisms actually apply to your deployment environment, and coat only if one or more failure modes has a realistic probability of occurring during your product's warranty period.
